A City Paused: How COVID Reshaped Crime in San Francisco

In early 2020, San Francisco, like much of the world, came to an abrupt halt. Offices closed, tourism disappeared, and everyday routines were disrupted almost overnight. Because many types of crime depend on movement and social interaction, this moment provides a natural experiment: what happens to reported crime when a city suddenly slows down? Using police incident data, this story focuses on the period from 2019 to 2022 to examine how reported crime changed across time, space, and category.

A sudden break in activity

The first question is not simply whether crime increased or decreased, but how quickly patterns shifted when urban life was disrupted.

Figure 1: Monthly number of reported crimes in San Francisco from 2019 to 2022. The shaded area marks the COVID-19 lockdown period beginning in March 2020.

The change is immediate and pronounced. At the onset of lockdown in early 2020, reported incidents drop sharply, reaching their lowest levels within a few months. This is not a gradual trend, but a clear structural break in the data. This pattern supports the idea that many crimes depend on routine activity — people moving through public space, interacting in commercial areas, and participating in everyday urban life. When those interactions are reduced, opportunities for certain types of crime decline as well.

However, the figure should be interpreted carefully. Police incident data does not measure crime directly, but recorded events. Changes in reporting behavior, policing priorities, and access to public services during the pandemic may also contribute to the observed decline. As a result, the drop reflects changes in reported incidents, not necessarily a complete reduction in all forms of crime.

The two maps show that, despite the overall drop in incidents, the spatial pattern remains broadly consistent. The same areas of the city continue to appear as hotspots before and during COVID, suggesting that the underlying geography of reported crime is relatively stable.

This indicates that while the volume of incidents changed, the structure of where crime is concentrated did not shift dramatically. Urban features such as population density, commercial activity, and transport corridors appear to continue shaping where incidents are recorded, even during a period of reduced mobility.

At the same time, any differences between the maps should be interpreted cautiously. These visualizations are descriptive and aggregate many observations. They cannot distinguish between changes in underlying behavior and changes in reporting or policing practices across neighborhoods.

Not all crime behaves the same

A decline in total incidents does not mean all types of crime respond in the same way.

Figure 3: Monthly incident counts for the most common crime categories from 2019 to 2022.

The category-level trends show clear differences in how crime types respond to disruption. Theft-related incidents, in particular, drop sharply during the lockdown period, aligning with reduced movement and fewer opportunities for interaction in public spaces. In contrast, other categories show smaller declines or recover more quickly.

This uneven response highlights that crime is not a single phenomenon. Different categories depend on different conditions, some are highly sensitive to mobility and opportunity, while others are less affected by changes in public activity.

The interactive format reinforces this point by allowing individual categories to be isolated. This makes it clear that the pandemic did not produce a uniform effect, but rather reshaped the composition of reported incidents.

Conclusion

The COVID-19 pandemic did not simply reduce crime in San Francisco — it reorganized it.

The time series reveals a sharp break in reported incidents, reflecting a sudden disruption of everyday urban activity. The spatial comparison shows that, despite this drop, crime remained concentrated in familiar areas of the city. Finally, the category-level analysis demonstrates that different types of crime responded differently to the same disruption.

Together, these patterns suggest that reported crime is closely tied to the rhythms of city life. When movement, interaction, and routine activity change, the opportunities for different types of incidents change as well.

At the same time, these findings must be interpreted with care. Police incident data reflects not only behavior, but also reporting practices, institutional priorities, and enforcement decisions. Understanding these limitations is essential to interpreting the patterns responsibly.
